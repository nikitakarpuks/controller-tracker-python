● There are two compounding issues:

  1. focal_px = max(fx, fy) — small systematic overestimate

  Using the maximum instead of the geometric mean sqrt(fx*fy) slightly inflates expected_px. Minor unless fx and fy differ significantly.

  2. The fundamental mismatch: physical projection vs threshold-based measurement

  The formula computes:
  expected_px = focal * led_radius_mm / depth   # physical disc projection

  But blob detection returns:
  sqrt(cv2.contourArea(cnt) / π)   # equivalent-circle radius at the detection threshold

  These measure completely different things. expected_px is the radius of the LED's physical image on the sensor. blob_radius is the radius of the
  region where pixel intensity exceeded pixel_threshold (or pass2_threshold if pass-2 is active). The detected region is the bright core, not the
  physical disc size.

  3. Pass-2 adaptive threshold compounds it at close range

  At close range, depth decreases, so expected_px grows as 1/depth. But simultaneously, because the LEDs appear brighter at close range,
  pass2_threshold is driven up adaptively — so the detected blob shrinks (only the very bright core is above the higher threshold). The
  _correct_radii_for_threshold function tries to compensate using sqrt(ln(P/thr1) / ln(P/thr2)), but:
  - It only corrects back to pass-1 level, not to physical size
  - It assumes a Gaussian profile which may not hold for saturated pixels

  The net result: as depth decreases, expected_px goes up and blob_radius goes down simultaneously. At close range the ratio blob_radius /
  expected_px can easily fall below 0.1, explaining why 0.3 was too tight.

  The pragmatic fix is exactly what you did — calibrate blob_size_min_factor (and possibly led_radius_mm) empirically to match what blobs actually
  measure across the depth range you care about, rather than relying on the physical model.

  Why per-controller thresholds are the right framing:

  ref_brightness for pass-2. The current code collapses them into one value at the 25th percentile, throwing away that structure.

  ---
  Proposed solution: bimodal split → two separate pass-2 detections, merged spatially

  Step 1 — detect the split.
  After collecting mean_vals from pass-1, check if the brightness distribution is bimodal. A robust way without full k-means: sort mean_vals, find
  the largest gap between consecutive sorted values (or check if np.percentile(mean_vals, 75) / np.percentile(mean_vals, 25) > split_factor, e.g.

  enter the final result as-is.
